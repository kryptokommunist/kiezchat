# Substance-Related Emergencies (First Aid adjacent)

Drug use always carries risks and can lead to emergencies, even when safer use guidelines are followed. In some situations, it can be helpful to know what someone has taken. However, this is not decisive, because first aid is always administered based on symptoms. On this page we have compiled information and tips that can help you intervene in an emergency.

Don't be afraid of doing something wrong. Someone needs your help, and doing nothing or looking away can be (life-)threatening for the affected person or persons involved.

If necessary and possible: ask other people to help you.


## PROCEDURE WHEN FINDING A PERSON

Stay calm.

Secure the scene: Remove hazards (electricity, broken glass, etc.) without putting yourself at risk.

**Check consciousness**: Speak to the person (loudly), shake them, and if necessary apply a pain stimulus (e.g. pinch the inside of their upper arm gently).

**Check breathing**: If the person is unconscious, check whether they are breathing (e.g. look for the rise and fall of the chest, feel for a breath of air on your cheek).

If the person is unconscious, not breathing, or medical assistance is needed for any other reason, call **emergency services** immediately: **radio or [+49 171 2124844](tel:) (for Kiez Burn 2026)**

Emergency call — the "5 Ws": 

* WHERE did it happen?
* WHAT happened?
* HOW MANY people are injured?
* WHAT INJURIES do they have?
* WAIT for follow-up questions.

Administer first aid according to the symptoms until the medics arrives.

When emergency personnel arrive, inform them (they are bound by medical confidentiality!) of any substances that were consumed (if known). This is especially important if painkillers or anaesthetics need to be administered, and in cases of opioid overdose.


### CIRCULATORY SHOCK

Shock can be life-threatening, as the brain and other organs may no longer receive sufficient oxygen. It can occur, for example, after overdoses of downers (= sedating and sleep-inducing substances), as a result of allergic reactions, following severe psychological stress, or after physical overexertion.

*Symptoms*: pale, ashen complexion; cold sweat; restlessness; disorientation; dizziness; nausea; possible brief loss of consciousness.

*Procedure*: Lay the person on their back. Monitor breathing and consciousness. If the person falls unconscious move to "Unconsciousness"


### UNCONSCIOUSNESS

Consuming high doses (mainly downers, e.g. alcohol, GHB/GBL, heroin, benzos) can impair consciousness so severely that the affected person can no longer be roused even by strong external stimuli. This state can also occur as a result of shock. Unconscious persons are helpless and may be at risk of suffocation or cardiac arrest.

*Symptoms*: The person is breathing but is not conscious.

*Procedure*: Place the person in the recovery position, call an emergency doctor, keep the person warm with blankets or similar, and continue monitoring their breathing. If they regain consciousness: do not give them food — this can lead to suffocation if they lose consciousness again.

Recovery Position:

* If the person is wearing glasses, remove them.
* Turn the person onto their back.
* Kneel beside the person and place the arm closest to you at a right angle, extended away from the body (palm facing upward).
* Cross the far arm over the person's chest and place the back of their hand against their cheek; hold the hand in this position.
* Bend the far leg and roll the person toward you onto their side. (Pregnant persons must be turned onto their left side to prevent the unborn child from pressing on the vena cava and restricting blood flow to the mother's heart.)
* Make sure the person cannot roll onto their back or stomach, and that no pressure is placed on the chest.
* Tilt the person's head back and angle it toward the ground — the mouth must face downward so that saliva, blood and vomit can drain away and are not inhaled.
* Stay with the person and make sure they continue breathing and remain in a stable position.


### CARDIAC / RESPIRATORY ARREST

Cardiac or respiratory arrest causes oxygen deprivation in the brain, which can lead to brain damage or death within a short time. It can be caused by, among other things, the (combined) use of downers (e.g. alcohol, GHB/GBL, heroin, benzos), which slow the body's functions (cardiovascular, respiratory, muscular activity).

*Symptoms*: The person is unconscious and not breathing.

*Procedure*: Call emergency services immediately and perform chest compressions and rescue breaths in rapid alternation (30 compressions followed by 2 rescue breaths), continuing until the ambulance arrives or breathing resumes. If two people are available, they can alternate between breaths and compressions. If you are alone and/or unsure, the German Red Cross recommends performing chest compressions only and no rescue breaths, as pumping alone still delivers some oxygen to the lungs.

* Chest compressions: The person must be lying on a firm, hard surface. Place both palms one on top of the other in the center of the chest (on the lower third of the breastbone). Spread your fingers away from the chest and press the breastbone straight down with straight arms — firmly and briefly (3–5 cm deep) at a rate slightly faster than one beat per second (approximately 80–100 times per minute). Note: ribs may break during chest compressions — this is not a reason to stop! A few broken ribs are preferable to permanent brain damage.
* Rescue breaths (mouth-to-nose / mouth-to-mouth): Lay the unconscious person on their back and kneel beside them. Remove any foreign objects from the mouth and tilt the head back — otherwise the tongue will block the airway. Push the lower jaw upward and close the mouth or nose. Take a deep breath and exhale into the person's nose or mouth as you normally would. Check whether the chest rises.


### SEIZURE (E.G. EPILEPTIC SEIZURE)

*Symptoms*: Eyes rolling upward; muscles going rigid; arms and legs jerking violently (possible loss of bladder or bowel control).

*Procedure*: Protect the person from injury (move objects away, pad sharp edges). Do not attempt to restrain the person or place any object between their teeth. If the seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes or it is the person's first seizure: call an emergency doctor! After the seizure, place the person in the recovery position and check breathing and pulse. The person should see a doctor afterwards.


### PSYCHOMOTOR SEIZURE

Can occur when stimulants (e.g. speed, crystal meth, or MDMA) are taken in excessive doses and may last over 30 minutes.

*Symptoms*: Impaired perception; purposeless, difficult or uncontrollable automatic movements or actions (chewing, picking, scratching, tapping, standing up/sitting down, head swaying, etc.).

*Procedure*: Protect the person from injury (move objects away, pad sharp edges). If the person is not moving too vigorously, try to encourage them to sit down and attempt to calm them. If this state does not improve within 30 minutes or worsens: call emergency services.


### SEROTONIN SYNDROME

Serotonin syndrome occurs when there is too much of the neurotransmitter serotonin in the brain or other parts of the body. It can be caused, for example, by an excessively high dose of MDMA, or by combining MDMA with antidepressants, MAO inhibitors, or other substances that raise serotonin levels. Serotonin syndrome can be life-threatening.

*Symptoms*: Fever; chills; trembling; muscle twitching; diarrhoea; nausea; high blood pressure; loss of coordination; cardiac arrhythmia; agitation and confusion.

*Procedure*: If serotonin syndrome is suspected, call emergency services immediately and inform them of the substances consumed.


### HEATSTROKE

In heatstroke, the body temperature rises sharply and the body is no longer able to cool itself sufficiently (also due to lack of fluids). Causes include the (combined) use of stimulants (with alcohol), hours of dancing, inadequate fluid intake, and direct sun exposure.

*Symptoms*: Flushed, hot face; warm to very hot skin; rapid heartbeat; nausea.

*Procedure*: Take the person outside into fresh air and shade, or find a quieter, cooler spot indoors. Have the person sit down and give them water, vitamin drinks, and mineral drinks. If possible, cool specific areas of the body with damp cloths. Important: cooling down too quickly can trigger cardiac arrest! If there is no improvement, call emergency services! Monitor the person's breathing and consciousness.
