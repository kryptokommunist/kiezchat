# Lights workshop: Modify KB chain lights

*Hello lovely light seekers and welcome to this little Workshop instruction.* 

*Get a tea, sit down and relax. Input is coming now:*


\
The Goal of this Project is and was to extend the lifespan of existing KB Glas bottle lights and to upgrade them. Its an experiment and I just dismantle some light chains and rebuild 8 new light chains (**Zigbee version**).

**So we replace the glas bottles with plastic ones (HDPE) and make them waterproof (when we work correctly)**

\n\n*The conversion for a Light Chains takes about 3 man-hours in total (plus an extra half an hour if the bottles are resealed with heat-shrink tubing).*


\

# Tools you need


* wire cutter
* cable stripper
* heavy metall tool (for destroying the glas bottles)
* soldering station & solder
* Knife (It's best to have a hacksaw as well)
* long-nose-pliers / slot srewdriver
* step drill (for the lids)
* A heat gun, or at least a lighter

  \


# Material you need


* Bulb testing adapter (B22 to E27) (we have two)
* new bottles (HDPE)
* cable fittings (double entry, PG16)
* enough bulbs
* Duct tape, or better, large heat-shrink tubing (to reseal the bottles)
* a smaller heat-shrink tube to secure the earthing wire (PE)

# How to


*Generally speaking, I recommend preparing all the lids, including the cable entries, and cutting or sawing the bottles first. That way, you can then solder them in peace. It's easier and more efficient.* 


* get all the equipment (hehe) 

  \
  \
  \
  ## Disassemble the old chain lights

  ### 
* ### **Cut the cable above the glas bottle (unplug the lights first!)**

  So we reduce the risk of broken cable, bcs the cable inside the bottle is notted and so there is a higher risk of broken cable. We reduce this risk and dumb the inside cable.

  \
* ### D**estroy the glas bottles.**

  Get yourself a safety glas and gloves.

  Put the glas bottle in a box. Then use a heavy metall tool and carefully smash the glas bottle.

  After you opened it use a wire cutter to cut the inside cables. We want to get the bulb, **be carefully, the connection is very sensitive** - so we cut the wire and just get the bulb.

  After seperate the glas and cable trash and dumb it.

  \
  \
  \
  ## Preparatory work on the new chain lights

##  

* ### **Cut the HDPE bottles**

  \
  Carefully cut the bottle horizontally with a knife or a hacksaw. But **don't cut all the way through** – that way, it will be easier to close the bottle again afterwards. 

  *At first, I cut the bottles quite near the bottom. But it's better to cut them at the top. That way, the light shining through is less blocked.*


\
* ### **prepare the cable fittings**

  \
  Drill a hole in the lid with the step drill. **Dont drill to big,** otherwise it wont be waterproof!

  **When you drilled too big use hot glue to seal**

  Srew the cable fitting on this hole. Use a long-nose-pliers or a slot srewdriver to hold the ring. So you can screw it tight.


\
* ### Cut the heat-shrink tubing

  \
  If you're using the nice shrink tubing to reseal the bottles rather than just sticky tape, cut it to size beforehand. To do this, mark down the desired spacing on a wooden board and then cut strips to size (I went for 4 cm).

  \
  \
  \
  ## Build the new chain lights

  \
  \
* ### Connect the two cables of one light

  \
  Put the two cables into the cable fiitting. Its a bit tricky, the sealing of the fitting is very tight (somehow it have to) 

  I used a screwdriver to remove the sealing and insert the cables seperately through them.

  \
  Then take a knife and **strip the first 6 cm or so** of each cable. Don't cut too deep, so as not to damage the wires (if you do, simply cut right down to the damaged section and start again).

  \
  Take a small **cable tie** and use it to bundle the two cables together. This provides extra tension relief and also makes soldering easier later on.

  \
  ![](/api/attachments.redirect?id=f36c7632-1465-4a68-a423-56913f23ecf7 " =1280x960")

  \
  \
  \
  \
* ### solder the two cables together

  \
  First, solder the two PE wires (earth) (green-yellow) together. 

  To do this, strip the insulation from the two green-yellow wires (in this case, the cable was only yellow). Take care not to damage the wires. A wire stripper works best for this, but a side cutter will also do if used carefully. 

  Then slide a piece of the thin heat-shrink tubing, about 3 cm long, over one end and solder the two wires together. Here's a good, short explanatory video:

  \
  [https://youtu.be/NSqPHQ1zQco](https://youtu.be/NSqPHQ1zQco)

  \
  \
  \
  Once you've stripped the insulation from the remaining wires, twist the two blue wires together and the two black (or brown) wires together. Then solder them using solder, as shown in the previous video.\n*It doesn't need to be that long; about 3 cm is enough (that way, the bulb won't hang down quite so low later on, and **it'll look better**)*

  \
  Next, add a nice amount of solder to the contacts on the LED bulb so that the wires connect properly. 

  Then **solder the wires to the bulb** as shown in the video. **It is very important that the two contacts on the bulb do not touch each other!** Otherwise, there will be a short circuit and the bulbs will be damaged.

  \
  *Hold the soldering iron between the solder on the cable wires and the light bulb. This will heat them both up and ensure they connect well together*

  \
  [soldering bulb.mp4 720x1280](/api/attachments.redirect?id=7a5d237f-502a-4e04-a443-071826cc7920)



\

* ### Hot glue

  ![](/api/attachments.redirect?id=b0db8691-b722-4af5-9034-b3c84f6c2e90 "right-50 =272x640")

  Once the soldering is complete, seal the exposed contacts of the light bulb using a hot-glue gun. 

  This will provide some protection against any water that might find its way in (it probably won't be completely watertight, as small channels are likely to remain) and, above all, **it acts as protection for the contacts by providing strain relief! (This is also very important, as otherwise the weight of the bulb would pull the contacts off.)**


\

\

\

* ### Labeling

  \

 ![](/api/attachments.redirect?id=cac7c80c-5008-4e24-9c07-bfa3fc783bea "left-50 =272x640")

The light bulbs use **Zigbee** (newer models use Bluetooth) and can be **controlled individually**. The light colour can also be customised.


And so that you know which bulb is which afterwards, they're **all numbered**. So use a waterproof pen to write the bulb's number on the bottle. Feel free to write it several times. It seems to me that the ink on the bottle is easier to wipe off, so I've also written it on the lid.


\

\

* ### testing the light chains

  \
  Then plug in the fairy lights and check that all the bulbs are actually working. 

  Once we've sealed the solder joints, it's safe.

  *(I did this carefully before using the hot glue gun – DANGER TO LIFE!)*

  \


* ### re-sealing the bottles

  \
  Now that everything's going well, it's time to seal the bottle again. To do this, use the clear sticky tape, or a nicer, fancier method (such as my heat-shrink tubing, for example)


\
* ### packaging the light chains

  \
  The final step is to pack the light chains neatly back into the boxes, **so that the next users can easily use them again.** 

  \
  To do this, we roll up the cables between the lights individually and tie them together with a reusable cable tie or a piece of cable/wire etc. (no tape – that gets sticky).

  \
  The chain lights are then placed in the boxes: one on the left, one on the right (separated by a piece of cardboard or similar) and one on top (ideally with a divider between them as well).

  \

# Room for improvements


* Improve storrage, buy reusable cable ties or small belt to tie the cable of every light

  \


* **Best would be to use a real light fitting (B22)**

  The bulbs are not made to solder them directly on the cable. It works, specially with the hot glue (stabilized), but is not the best option.


* When the bottles are heavy enough to have the straight after hanging its also **possible to remove the bottom** and let it open. So inside water could run out, temperature is no problem anymore and its much nicer without the tape (or heat-shrink tubing)

  *When we work carefully its also possible to remove the bottom even without hanging straight. But we need to drill holes inside the bottle, so the water can run out. Better to improve the connection of the grounding cable (yellow, PE)* 


* I guess the lovely white heat-shrink tubing gets dirty very quickly :D 

  Perhaps it would have been better to use **black heat-shrink tubing** for resealing the bottles. It could also be made to look even nicer using, for example, riveted steel plate.

# To check during KB26


* **Temperature**

  \
  The lights are getting hot and running all day. 

  During my test they became 75 degrees Celsius.

  So I didnt used normal PE bottles. HDPE is more temperature safe, but its still not the best and specially with the sun during the summer time at KB it could be too much.

  \-> when temperature will be too high we can drill one or two holes into the bottle for circulation

* **Waterproofness**

  \
  Are the new bottles really waterproof?

  The fittings are great, *when screw them really thight..*

  And the tape for sealing the bottle is a weak spot.

  \
  → exept to fix these both weak spot its also possible to drill a few holes into the bottle to let the water out. (both ends of the bottle)

  Because of the hot glue at the soldering its quite water proof there (never to 100% but its ok when the water wont fill the bottle.. )


* **labeling of the lights**

  \
  Every light is numbered, programmed, so you can run them individually. 

  Normally its just a number, The replacement bulbs have a A + a number (eg. A12)

  The number is on the bulbs and is written with a waterproof pen on the HDPE bottle.

  **Check if the labeling is still good visible.**
