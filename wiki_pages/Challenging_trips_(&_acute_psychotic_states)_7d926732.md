# Challenging trips (& acute psychotic states)

Every year people at Kiez Burn people will use psychedelics, many for the first time. Some of these psychedelic experiences will be difficult ones. Even though society has termed these experiences "bad trips," a difficult psychedelic experience is not necessarily a bad one. The challenging situation can be seen as an opportunity for transformation.

A psychedelic experience is influenced by the set and setting. Set refers to the drug as well as the mindset of the person taking it. What is the dose, duration, and purity of the drug? What is the person's intention, their emotional state, and their previous experience with the substance? Setting refers to the physical environment: noise, lighting, other people, familiarity, safety. Changes in set and setting can radically shift a psychedelic experience. 


### CHALLENGING TRIPS

Challenging trips can occur when someone is overwhelmed by the effects of one or more substances — usually psychedelics (e.g. LSD, mushrooms, 2C-B). This situation can also be overwhelming for you as a first aider. Respect your own limits and don't do anything that makes you uncomfortable. If you need support, ask for help.

*Symptoms*: Disorientation; panic; fear; feeling overwhelmed by the situation; etc.

*Procedure*: At Kiez Burn, we base our approach on the principles that were established by the "Zendo Project" (peer support & harm reduction).


1. Create a safe space – Try to create a calm environment, most importantly, be being a calm presence. Ask what would make them most comfortable. Offer blankets and water. Ask permission before touching someone in any way. Keep personal information in confidence.
2. Sitting, not guiding - Be a calm meditative presence of acceptance, compassion, and caring. Promote feelings of trust and security. Let the person's unfolding experience be the guide. Don't try to get ahead of the process. Explore distressing issues as they emerge, but simply being with the person can provide support. 
3. Talk through, not down – Without distracting from the experience, help the person connect with what they are feeling. Invite the person to take the opportunity to explore what's happening and encourage them to try not to resist it. 
4. Difficult is not the same as bad – Challenging experiences can wind up being our most valuable, and may lead to learning and growth. Consider that it may be happening for an important reason. Suggest that they approach the fear and difficult aspects of their experience with curiosity

### Acute psychotic states

Prolonged and/or high-dose use of stimulants (e.g. speed, crystal meth, cocaine) often leaves the body and mind with little opportunity to recover adequately. Sleep deprivation and the associated mental exhaustion make the occurrence of temporary psychotic states more likely.

*Symptoms*: Increased irritability; visual or auditory hallucinations; paranoia or persecution delusions. This typically persists until the brain has had sufficient rest.

*Procedure*: Try to remain calm and explain to the person that their situation is a natural reaction of the brain, and that such symptoms can occur in all people - even due to acute sleep deprivation without substance use. Emphasise that this state will generally pass once they rest and get enough sleep, and that they do not need to fear becoming permanently "unwell" — the "fear of fear itself" often leads people to spiral deeper into this state.

Work with the person to find out what might help them calm down (e.g. a change of scenery, warm drinks — no coffee or energy drinks, calming breathing techniques, close friends). If this state persists for too long and becomes truly unbearable for the person, offer to call an emergency doctor. Emergency doctors can administer sedatives that can stop the anxious thoughts and place the person in deep sleep for a few hours.
